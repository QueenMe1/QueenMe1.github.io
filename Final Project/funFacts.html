Currency

The naira (₦) is the official currency of Nigeria, introduced in 1973 to replace the British pound. It is issued and regulated by the Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) and is used for all daily transactions across the country. The naira reflects Nigeria’s history, leadership, and economy through the people and symbols featured on its banknotes.

₦5 Naira

The ₦5 note features Alhaji Sir Abubakar Tafawa
Balewa, Nigeria’s first Prime Minister. He played
a major role in leading Nigeria
to independence in 1960. The note
symbolizes Nigeria’s early political leadership.

₦10 Naira

The ₦10 note shows Dr. Alvan Ikoku,
a respected educator and nationalist.
He contributed greatly to the
development of modern education in Nigeria.
His image represents learning and intellectual growth.

₦20 Naira

The ₦20 note features General Murtala Ramat
Muhammed, a former Head of State. He is
remembered for his decisive leadership and anti-
corruption reforms. The note reflects patriotism
and national unity.

₦50 Naira

The ₦50 naira note does not show a single
person. Instead, it features three Nigerians in traditional
attire, representing the three major ethnic
groups in Nigeria: Hausa, Yoruba, and Igbo.
The note symbolizes cultural diversity,
unity, and national identity across the country.

₦100 Naira

The ₦100 note features Chief Obafemi Awolowo
as well. This note emphasizes his lasting
influence on Nigeria’s political and economic development.
It symbolizes vision and leadership.

₦200 Naira

The ₦200 note shows Alhaji Sir Ahmadu Bello,
the Sardauna of Sokoto. He was a key leader
in Northern Nigeria and a strong advocate for unity.
The note represents regional leadership and nation-building.

₦500 Naira

The ₦500 note features Dr. Nnamdi Azikiwe,
Nigeria’s first President. He was a
major figure in the independence movement
and journalism. The note reflects freedom,
nationalism, and intellectual activism.

₦1000 Naira

The ₦1000 naira note features the portraits of
Alhaji Aliyu Mai-Bornu and Dr. Clement Isong.
Both men served as former Governors of
the Central Bank of Nigeria and
played key roles in shaping Nigeria’s financial system.
The note represents economic leadership,
monetary stability, and national development.

Traditional Dances

Atilogwu (Igbo - SouthEast Nigeria)

Atilogwu is a fast-paced, energetic dance
traditionally performed by Igbo youth.
It is known for its acrobatic moves, high jumps,
and rhythmic footwork that showcase
strength and agility. The dance
is often performed
at festivals, celebrations,and cultural showcases.

Bata (Yoruba - SouthWest Nigeria)

Bata is a traditional Yoruba dance
closely tied to sacred drum rhythms.
The movements are powerful and expressive,
mirroring the talking drums used in the performance.
It is commonly performed during
religious ceremonies and royal events.

Ekombi (Efik - Cross River State)

Ekombi is a graceful dance performed
mainly by young women of the
Efik people. It emphasizes elegance, controlled
movements, and expressive hand gestures.
The dance often reflects themes
of beauty, courtship, and femininity.

Swange Dance (Tiv – Benue State)

Swange is a rhythmic and energetic dance
popular among the Tiv people.
Dancers move their shoulders, waist, and
feet in synchronized patterns to drum beats and
songs. It is usually performed at social gatherings,
festivals, and communal celebrations.

Koroso Dance (Hausa – Northern Nigeria)

Koroso is a traditional Hausa dance
that combines drumming, singing, and
energetic movements. It is often performed by
young men and women during festivals
and important community events.
The dance highlights unity, storytelling, and cultural pride.

Sharo (Fulani – Northern Nigeria)

Sharo is a ceremonial Fulani dance
associated with rites of passage and endurance.
It often accompanies the Sharo festival,
where young men demonstrate bravery and self-control.
The dance reflects values of strength, honor, and resilience.
